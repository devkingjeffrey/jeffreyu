Reconciling Multi-Branch Donor & Partner Data Across a National NGO Network

Built from real, recurring patterns observed across national and pan-African NGOs

The Situation

A composite, typical of what shows up across national NGOs with semi-autonomous branches: a secretariat in Abuja running programmes through a dozen-plus state or regional chapters, each reporting on whatever tool was locally available — one branch on spreadsheets, another on a paper log transcribed monthly, a couple on a shared drive with no shared format. A donor reporting deadline arrives, and the secretariat's programme team spends days manually reconciling submissions that don't share field names, date formats, or even currency conventions, before anyone can produce one clean number for a funder.

The Architecture

Built on Salesforce Non-Profit Success Pack (NPSP), structured around the Household/Organization Account model already native to NPSP — the national secretariat as a parent Account, each branch or partner as a child Account linked through the standard Account hierarchy, so branch-level activity rolls up to a single national view without losing branch-level detail.

Programme and grant data sit on custom objects related to each branch Account, rather than living in disconnected spreadsheets — this is what turns "branch X's numbers" into "one filterable slice of the same dataset."

Incoming records from branches (via Data Loader or Data Import Wizard for bulk imports) pass through Duplicate and Matching Rules configured on the key identifying fields — donor name, grant ID, reporting period — so records entered inconsistently across branches get flagged or merged rather than silently creating parallel entries. Flow-based validation catches the most common breakage points directly: mismatched date formats, missing required fields, and currency inconsistencies get caught at entry, not at reconciliation time three days before a donor deadline.

Reporting sits on top of this as report types and dashboards scoped by branch, region, or donor — giving the secretariat one live, consolidated view instead of a manual roll-up exercise every reporting cycle.

What This Would Change

Framed as expected outcomes of the architecture, not measured results of a real engagement: reconciliation before a donor report shifts from a manual, multi-day cross-check to a live report anyone on the team can pull. Duplicate donor and grant records stop accumulating silently across branches. And the secretariat gains a single source of truth it can hand to an auditor or funder without needing to explain which spreadsheet is the "real" one.

Milestone-Gated Fund Release for a Multi-Country Grant Cycle

Built from real, recurring patterns observed across grant-making and fellowship-running NGOs

The Situation

A composite, typical of an organization running a grant cycle or fellowship where sub-grantees or cohort members are spread across several countries or regions, and funds are meant to release only as each recipient hits a defined milestone. In practice, the compliance team is stuck manually checking uploaded reports against a spreadsheet of milestone criteria before every disbursement, and different program officers apply the criteria slightly differently — some recipients get paid before their documentation is actually complete, others get held up for reasons that were never written down anywhere consistent.

The Architecture

Built on Salesforce NPSP, with each grant or fellowship modeled as an Opportunity (or a custom Grant object where the standard Opportunity fields don't fit cleanly), broken into stages that mirror the actual milestone structure — application, approval, milestone 1 delivered, milestone 2 delivered, closed. Each stage transition is gated by an Approval Process, so a disbursement can't move forward until the required documentation fields are populated and a reviewer has formally signed off — replacing "did someone remember to check" with a rule the system enforces every time.

Validation rules on the milestone fields catch incomplete submissions before they ever reach a reviewer's queue, and a Flow handles the notification side — automatically alerting the program officer and the recipient's point of contact the moment a stage changes, instead of relying on someone remembering to send an update email.

Because every grantee sits on a standard Account/Contact structure, the same reporting layer used for donor-side reconciliation (see the pattern above) applies here too — a dashboard showing exactly which recipients are pending, approved, or paid, filterable by country, cohort, or funding source.

What This Would Change

Framed as expected outcomes of the architecture, not measured results of a real engagement: milestone criteria stop living in one compliance officer's head or a personal spreadsheet, and instead become a rule the system applies the same way every time. Disbursement timing becomes consistent across program officers rather than varying by who's reviewing the file. And when a donor or auditor asks "show me exactly why this recipient was paid on this date," there's a system-enforced approval trail to point to, not a reconstructed explanation after the fact.

Automating Multi-Site Staff Onboarding During a Hiring Surge

Built from real, recurring patterns observed across NGOs scaling personnel across multiple sites at once

The Situation

A composite, typical of an org hiring rapidly across several offices or field sites at once — a central HR lead trying to onboard new staff into multiple locations in parallel, with each site's paperwork, equipment provisioning, and system access requests tracked over email threads. New hires start before their access is ready, HR loses track of who's actually been fully onboarded versus who's still mid-process, and nobody has one place to see status across all sites at a glance.

The Architecture

Built on HubSpot, using a custom onboarding pipeline (built on Deals or a custom object, depending on the account tier) with a stage for every step of the process — offer signed, documentation received, IT/systems access requested, site orientation scheduled, fully onboarded. Each stage change triggers a Workflow that automatically notifies the relevant party — IT when access needs provisioning, the site lead when someone's ready for orientation — instead of HR manually chasing each handoff by email.

A Slack integration mirrors key stage changes into a shared onboarding channel, so anyone on the team can see status in real time without opening HubSpot directly — the same "default to open and visual" principle that shows up elsewhere in how the org already wants to operate.

A simple dashboard rolls up onboarding status by site, giving HR one view of exactly who's stuck at which stage across every location, instead of reconstructing that picture from separate email threads each time someone asks.

What This Would Change

Framed as expected outcomes of the architecture, not measured results of a real engagement: new hires stop starting before their access and equipment are ready, because the workflow surfaces the gap automatically instead of relying on someone remembering to follow up. HR gains one live view across all sites instead of piecing status together from inboxes. And the handoffs between HR, IT, and site leads happen on a system-enforced trigger, not a manually-sent email that might get missed during a hiring surge.

Secure Field Data Capture Without Reliable Connectivity

Built from real, recurring patterns observed across NGOs collecting sensitive data in the field

The Situation

A composite, typical of an org whose field staff collect sensitive case data — protection intake, legal casework, health records — in locations with unreliable internet, sometimes none at all. Today that means paper forms photographed and messaged in later, or a spreadsheet filled in from memory once someone's back at the office with signal. Details get lost or misremembered in the gap, and sensitive information sits unprotected on a phone's camera roll or a messaging app in the meantime.

The Architecture

Built using the Salesforce Mobile App with offline-capable forms — field staff fill in a structured record on their phone or tablet exactly as they would on paper, but the data is captured as clean, structured fields from the start rather than free text to be re-typed later. The device stores the record locally when there's no signal, and syncs automatically the moment a connection is available — no manual re-entry step, and nothing sits exposed in a messaging app while it waits to reach the system.

Field-level security and permission sets restrict who can see sensitive record types, so a case file is only visible to the people who actually need it — a basic but often-skipped control when data collection happens under time pressure in the field.

Once synced, the same record structure feeds directly into whatever reporting or case-tracking view the org already relies on, so field data doesn't need a separate manual transcription step to become usable centrally.

What This Would Change

Framed as expected outcomes of the architecture, not measured results of a real engagement: field staff capture cleaner data on the spot instead of reconstructing it from memory hours later. Sensitive records stay inside a permissioned system instead of living temporarily in a camera roll or chat thread. And headquarters gets usable, structured data the moment a device reconnects, instead of waiting on someone to manually transcribe a stack of photographed forms.

Automated Application Screening for a High-Volume Grant Cycle

Built from real, recurring patterns observed across NGOs running large-scale grant cycles, fellowships, and mentorship programs

The Situation

A composite, typical of an org running an annual grant cycle, fellowship, or mentorship program that draws thousands of applications. Every cycle, a small program team manually opens each submission to check basic eligibility — right country, right sector, required documents attached, budget within range — before anyone with real judgment even sees it. Reviewers' time goes to sorting instead of evaluating, and the cycle takes weeks longer than it should before a shortlist exists.

The Architecture

Built on Salesforce NPSP, with applications captured directly into the system through a web-to-lead style form rather than arriving as email attachments to be manually logged. A Flow runs an initial eligibility check against the structured fields the moment a submission comes in — required documents present, eligibility criteria met, budget within stated bounds — and automatically routes anything that fails a hard criterion to a rejection queue with the reason attached, rather than a reviewer discovering it three steps into reading the file.

Everything that passes the initial check lands in a reviewer queue, assigned automatically by sector or region using assignment rules, so applications get distributed evenly instead of piling up with whoever happens to check their inbox first.

For programs that need mentor/mentee or reviewer/applicant matching specifically, the same structured data — sector, seniority, stated goals — feeds a simple scoring or filtering view that surfaces the closest matches, cutting down the manual cross-referencing that pairing usually requires.

What This Would Change

Framed as expected outcomes of the architecture, not measured results of a real engagement: reviewers spend their time judging genuinely eligible applications instead of screening out ones that were never going to qualify. The shortlist stage starts sooner because eligibility is checked the moment a form is submitted, not batched into a manual review pass weeks later. And every rejected applicant has a clear, consistent reason on file, rather than an inconsistent judgment call made under time pressure.

Tracking Alumni & Long-Term Stakeholder Relationships Through Staff Turnover

Built from real, recurring patterns observed across NGOs running leadership programs, fellowships, and alumni networks

The Situation

A composite, typical of an org running a leadership program, fellowship, or long-standing donor network where the real value is in relationships tracked over years, not months. The person who knew "this graduate moved into a policy role two years after the program" or "this donor's last three gifts were tied to a specific project" leaves the org, and that context leaves with them — because it lived in their personal notes or memory, not in a shared system. A funder asks for multi-year impact evidence, and the answer depends on who's still around to remember it.

The Architecture

Built on Salesforce NPSP, using the standard Contact and Relationship objects to track not just a person's basic details but their ongoing story — career moves, engagement history, gift history, program milestones — all logged as Activities or custom timeline records tied to their Contact record, rather than living in one staff member's inbox or notebook.

Simple automation (Flow-based reminders) prompts staff to log a check-in or update after key touchpoints — a conference encounter, a career update heard secondhand, a renewed gift — so the record stays current without depending on any one person's memory or diligence.

Reporting rolls this up into a longitudinal view — career trajectories across a cohort, gift patterns across years — that a program director can pull directly, instead of reconstructing it by asking around the office.

What This Would Change

Framed as expected outcomes of the architecture, not measured results of a real engagement: institutional memory survives staff turnover because it lives in the system, not in someone's head. Multi-year impact evidence becomes a report you can generate on demand instead of a research project every time a funder asks. And relationship history stops depending on which staff member happens to remember a particular donor or graduate.
